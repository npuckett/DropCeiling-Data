Data & Findings

An open record of an adaptive light that learned over fifty-four days.

DropCeiling is twelve LED panels in a Toronto storefront window that respond to pedestrians — with the run data, the diagrams, and the findings all preserved and reproducible. This site is the analysis; the live installation is at thedropceiling.com.

Architecture

The whole system, in pieces.

The A-series diagrams (Mermaid-rendered Mermaid, or static SVG where Mermaid is weaker) cover the system end to end: the pipeline, the three nested loops, the meta-parameters, the modes, the trend windows, the self-tuning feedback, the budget, the database, real-time ingestion, and the web interface. For the long-form 18-section master reference, see the architecture page.

Three nested loops

The single most load-bearing explanation: three control loops at three timescales. Reaction (~33 ms — modes and gestures follow who is present now), anticipation (seconds–hours — trend windows pre-position the light), self-tuning (hours–days — the meta-review reshapes the twelve meta-parameters). The key sentence: only the innermost loop ever writes the light; each outer loop sets the terms for the one inside it.

Self-tuning feedback

PUSH (what the tuner senses) · RESIST (the friction stack: mean-reversion, step clamp, budget, value clamp) · META (the 3×/day review that re-governs the rules). A +0.050 nudge to energy becomes a +0.012 step after friction. The greyscale version (A7-bw) is the canonical ACADIA submission figure.

The output funnel

The strongest single idea: a city corner's worth of context collapses, thirty times a second, to the state of one virtual point light (~9 values), from which each of 12 panels brightens by distance. Twenty-three million tracking events distilled live into twelve values of light.

The database

Three tiers: raw 48h, hourly (∞), daily report. A week of data the database pruned was recovered intact from the system's own daily reports — the tiered retention saving its own history.

Honesty as a feature

The data the site fetches carries provenance flags. The chart above already uses them (grey bars are estimated, warm bars are the report-recovered week). Three more notes you should know about the run:

1.
Calibration artifact (Jan 29 – Feb 2). An uncorrected tracking offset mapped ~97% of pedestrians into the active zone. The merge step estimates the active/passive split from a matched weekday+hour window after the Feb 12 fix; those 285 estimated hours are flagged in the JSON.
2.
Feb 3 – 9 gap, recovered. The database pruned the 7 days of raw events; the daily-report JSONs generated at the time preserved them. 168 hours were ingested from those reports (tagged src: "report" in the JSON; shown in warm in the chart above).
3.
One surviving meta-review. The "162 meta-reviews" count from earlier drafts is wrong — the audit table is empty in both source DBs. Only one row survives, dated Feb 13 10:23. See H2.
